A solo project, five years in the making

Built with chalk dust
and stubbornness.

Heron is an indie app made by one person who believed math education could be better. Here's the story.

I'm Luke — a mathematical physics graduate, former math tutor, and the only person who built this app. Every line of code, every UI decision, every equation interaction was made by me, in my own time, for five years.

The idea came from tutoring. I worked with high school students who were genuinely struggling with algebra — not because they weren't capable, but because the way it was being taught wasn't reaching them. I could explain how to rearrange an equation until I was blue in the face. Some students got it. Many didn't.

The ones who clicked were always the ones who could see it happening. Not just follow steps — but watch a term move, watch a sign flip, watch the equation balance itself in real time. I started sketching out an app on paper during uni lectures. Five years later, here it is.

How it happened

2021 — The idea

Tutoring high school students through algebra, I kept running into the same wall. Explaining wasn't enough. I needed a way to make equations physical. I started sketching out what that could look like on paper.

2021–2022 — First prototype

Built the first draggable algebra engine in Unity while finishing my mathematical physics degree. It was rough, buggy, and barely functional — but the core idea worked. Dragging a term across the equals sign and watching the sign flip: it just made sense in a way no textbook diagram ever had.

2023 — Expanding the scope

Added the unit circle tool after realising trigonometry had the same problem as algebra — students memorised values without ever feeling what sin and cos actually meant. A draggable point on a circle solved it immediately.

2024 — Scientific notation

The third tool came after watching a student confidently write 3.2 × 10⁴ but have no idea what it meant. A draggable decimal point — watching the exponent update in real time — turned an abstract notation into something intuitive.

2025–2026 — Launch

Five years of evenings and weekends. Heron is finally live. It's free, it always will be, and I genuinely hope it helps someone understand something they've been struggling with.

Why it's free

I didn't build this to make money. I built it because I care about math education, and I think good tools should be accessible to every student regardless of what school they go to or what their parents earn. Heron is free, has no ads, and collects no data.

That said, keeping an app on the App Store and Play Store does have a small cost — developer licences, hosting, and the odd cup of coffee at 1am while squashing a bug. If Heron has helped you or your student and you'd like to contribute something, I'd be genuinely grateful.

Heron of Alexandria

The app is named after Heron of Alexandria — a mathematician, engineer, and inventor who lived around 10 AD. He designed the first recorded steam engine, invented an early vending machine, and wrote algorithms for computing square roots that predate modern computing by nearly two thousand years.

What made Heron remarkable wasn't just his intellect. It was his instinct to make abstract ideas physical. He didn't just describe geometry — he built machines that demonstrated it. His formula for calculating the area of a triangle from its three side lengths — still called Heron's Formula — is taught in high schools worldwide to this day.

I named the app after him because that's what I'm trying to do: take abstract mathematics and make it something you can touch.

How to use the app

01

Algebra Engine

Type any equation into the input field. Each term becomes a draggable tile. Drag a term across the equals sign to move it — the sign flips automatically (+ becomes −, × becomes ÷). Tap a variable to substitute a value. Use the toolbar to apply index laws, trig identities, log rules, or complex number operations.

02

Unit Circle

Drag the point around the circumference of the circle. The sin and cos lines update in real time as you move. Try sweeping through all four quadrants and watch how the signs change — it's the fastest way to understand why sin is negative in the third quadrant.

03

Scientific Notation

Enter any number. Drag the decimal point left or right across the digits. The scientific notation updates live — the coefficient and exponent both change as you drag. It makes the relationship between decimal position and powers of ten completely intuitive.
